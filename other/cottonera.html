Cottonera Dialect

One of the dialects of the Maltese language is the Cottonera dialect, known to locals as Kottoneran. Many inhabitants of the Three Cities speak the local dialect, and thus roughly amount to 10,000 speakers.

The most distinctive feature of this dialect is its treatment of vowels i and u after the silent consonant għ. In Standard Maltese, and other dialects, these vowels are realized as diphthongs after għ. However, in most situations, they remain monophthongs in the Cottonera dialect.

The Vowel I after Għ

The vowel i after għ remains an /i/ as in the English fleece, instead of diphthongizing to /ai/ as in the English price.

English - my/mine
Standard Maltese - tiegħi [ˈtiɐɪ]
Cottonera dialect - tiegħi [ˈtiːɪ]
 
English - he curses
Standard Maltese - jidgħi [ˈjɪdɐɪ]
Cottonera dialect - jidgħi [ˈjɪdɪ]

English - with me
Standard Maltese - miegħi [ˈmiɐɪ]
Cottonera dialect - miegħi [ˈmiːɪ]

This dialectal change does not occur with the words għid (easter), erbgħin (forty), sebgħin (seventy), and disgħin (ninety).

The local poet from Senglea, Dwardu Cachia (1858–1907), formed part of the Xirka Xemija in 1882, an organization which formulated one of the first standardized versions of written Maltese. Moreover, Cachia wrote a poem about this very alphabet, in which he made use of the 4-line rhyme. Coincidentally, the ABCB rhyme of the second stanza only works if read in his Cottonera dialect.

"Ebda ħażż ma jibdel leħnu
fil-fonetiku, qed ingħid:   [ɪˈniːt], not [ɪˈnɐɪt]
Ga, Ge, Gi ibsin jinstemgħu
"Għax tgħid", ħadthom bla taħbit"   [tɐhˈbiːt]

—Dwardu Cachia (1858–1907)  

The Vowel U after Għ

The vowel u after għ remains an /u:/ as in the English goose, instead of diphthongizing to /au/ as in the English mouth.

English - his
Standard Maltese - tiegħu [ˈtiɐu]
Cottonera dialect - tiegħu [ˈtiːʊ]

English - sent
Standard Maltese - mibgħut [mɪˈbɐʊt]
Cottonera dialect - mibgħut [mɪˈbuːt]

English - a piece of wood
Standard Maltese - għuda [ˈɐʊdɐ]
Cottonera dialect - għuda [ˈuːdɐ]

English - we can/could
Standard Maltese - nistgħu [ˈnɪstɐʊ]
Cottonera dialect - nistgħu [ˈnɪstʊ]

English - we sell
Standard Maltese - nbigħu [mˈbiɐʊ]
Cottonera dialect - nbigħu [mˈbiːʊ]

English - with him
Standard Maltese - miegħu [ˈmiɐʊ]
Cottonera dialect - miegħu [ˈmiːʊ]

The Vowel E after Għ

Although in contemporary Maltese (21st Century), the combination għe sometimes produces an /a/ vowel, the Cottonera dialect has widely kept the /e~i/ realization comparable to Standard Maltese.

English - she remained
Standard Maltese - baqgħet [ˈbɐʔɛt]
Cottonera dialect - baqgħet [ˈbɐʔɛt] / [ˈbɐqɪt]
Contemporary Maltese - baqgħet [ˈbɐʔɐt]

English - she fell
Standard Maltese - waqgħet [ˈwɐʔɛt]
Cottonera dialect - waqgħet [ˈwɐʔɛt] / [ˈwɐqɪt]
Contemporary Maltese - waqgħet [ˈwɐʔɐt]

English - he tired them
Standard Maltese - għejjiehom [ɛjˈjiːɔm]
Cottonera dialect - għejjiehom [ɛjˈjiːɔm]
Contemporary Maltese - għejjiehom [ɐjˈjiːɔm]

The Consonant Q

In Cottonera, most notably among the eldest demographic of Senglea, the consonant q is still pronounced as a voiceless uvular plosive /q/, as its counterpart in Classical Arabic. This sound survived in Modern Maltese only through the Cottonera dialect, instead of being replaced with the Standard glottal stop /ʔ/. However, it is important to note that it is severely in decline.

English - never
Standard Maltese - qatt [ʔɐtt]
Cottonera dialect - qatt [qɐtt]

English - he told me
Standard Maltese - qalli [ˈʔɐllɪ]
Cottonera dialect - qalli [ˈqɐllɪ]

English - we reside
Standard Maltese - noqogħdu [nɔˈʔɔːdʊ]
Cottonera dialect - noqogħdu [nɔˈqɔːdʊ]

English - artichokes
Standard Maltese - qaqoċċ [ʔɐˈʔɔtʃtʃ]
Cottonera dialect - qaqoċċ [qɐˈqɔtʃtʃ]

English - poverty
Standard Maltese - faqar [ˈfɐʔɐr]
Cottonera dialect - faqar [ˈfɐqɐr]

English - he reached
Standard Maltese - laħaq [ˈlɐhɐʔ]
Cottonera dialect - laħaq [ˈlɐhɐq]
